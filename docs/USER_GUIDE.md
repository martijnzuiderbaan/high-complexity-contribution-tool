# User Guide v1

This guide explains how to use the current Markdown artifacts as a practical
workbook flow for the **High-Complexity Contribution Tool** / **Instrument
Complexiteit naar Bijdrage**.

The current repository is not a software product. It is a content package for
structured reflection, role-context scanning, and a shared role-design
conversation.

## 1. Purpose

Use the tool when a gifted, hoogbegaafde, or high-complexity adult and their
work context need a safer way to discuss sustainable contribution.

The central question is:

> Under what role and context conditions does complex capacity become useful,
> sustainable contribution?

The tool helps users move from vague tension to one bounded 30-day work
experiment. It does not prove giftedness, diagnose a person, score talent, or
decide employment outcomes.

## 3. Before You Start

Check whether this is the right situation for the tool.

Use it when:

- The issue is about role fit, contribution, autonomy, learning, complexity,
  quality dialogue, sparring, translation, energy, or boundaries.
- The adult can decide what to keep private.
- The employer-side participant can examine role and context conditions.
- A small 30-day experiment is realistic.
- The conversation can be held in good faith, or with a facilitator if needed.

Do not use it as the main tool when:

- The real question is diagnosis, IQ, disability, burnout, mental health,
  occupational health, legal status, formal accommodation, discipline, hiring,
  promotion, performance, or support entitlement.
- The employer wants a score, ranking, selection argument, or proof that someone
  deserves support.
- The situation is unsafe, adversarial, legally sensitive, or already
  disciplinary without specialist support.
- There are no concrete work examples yet.
- The proposed experiment would add hidden workload without protection.

## 4. Roles and Responsibilities

Adult:

- Reflect privately first.
- Choose concrete work examples.
- Decide what to keep private, what may be shared, and what requires support
  before sharing.
- Bring a practical request rather than a demand to diagnose or label the
  situation.

Manager or employer:

- Examine the role and context before judging the person.
- Distinguish legitimate constraints from avoidable friction.
- Offer realistic role levers, not unlimited adaptation.
- Avoid using the output for hiring, promotion, ranking, performance judgment,
  or support entitlement.

HR partner, coach, or facilitator:

- Protect the boundaries of the tool.
- Help both sides stay concrete and non-diagnostic.
- Watch for over-disclosure, unsafe dynamics, formal HR issues, health-adjacent
  issues, or legal/occupational-health questions.
- Keep the session focused on one testable role experiment.

## 5. The Six Fit Dimensions

All three artifacts use the same six fit dimensions.

| Dimension | Practical question |
|---|---|
| Complexity fit | Is the work complex, systemic, ambiguous, non-routine, and cognitively engaging enough? |
| Autonomy fit | Is there enough freedom in method, sequencing, problem framing, and decision-making within legitimate boundaries? |
| Learning fit | Does the role offer continuing growth, novelty, mastery, and development after the initial learning curve? |
| Values and quality fit | Can quality, logic, fairness, ethics, integrity, and meaningful standards be discussed seriously? |
| Sparring and translation fit | Is there enough intellectual sparring, and can complex thinking be translated into language others can use? |
| Energy and boundary fit | Is the work sustainable in cognitive load, emotional load, sensory load, meeting load, focus time, recovery, and boundaries? |

Use these dimensions as prompts for evidence. Do not use them as a scorecard.

## 6. Individual Reflection Flow

Use the private and adult-facing sections of `docs/WORKBOOK_v1.md`.

1. Read the purpose and boundaries.
2. Fill in the quick summary lightly before going deep.
3. Work through the six dimensions.
4. For each prompt, mark a signal only if it helps you think. The important part
   is the concrete work example.
5. Notice the strongest pattern, such as hidden potential, underchallenge,
   overdrive risk, conflict or exit risk, or sustainable contribution.
6. Complete the disclosure planning table.
7. Write a minimum useful request you could safely make.
8. Complete the shareable conversation summary only if it is safe and useful.

Good adult preparation answers:

- "The strongest contribution appears when I can analyze ambiguous client
  issues before the solution is fixed."
- "The largest fit gap is method autonomy: outcomes are clear, but the process
  is controlled in ways that block useful judgment."
- "I am not asking to avoid constraints. I am asking to separate necessary
  constraints from method choices that could be left open."
- "A practical 30-day experiment could be clearer outcome boundaries plus more
  method freedom on one specific workstream."

Avoid:

- "This proves I am gifted."
- "The manager must accept my full interpretation."
- "The organization needs to redesign everything."
- "The output should go into a performance or HR file without discussion."

## 7. Employer or Manager Flow

Use `docs/MANAGER_CONVERSATION_GUIDE_v1.md` and the shared work-context
sections of `docs/WORKBOOK_v1.md`.

1. Read the purpose and boundaries.
2. Complete the quick role summary from the role-context side.
3. Work through the six dimensions using examples from the role, not assumptions
   about the person's identity.
4. Review the leadership and context conditions.
5. Mark relevant context modifiers, such as formal hierarchy, safety-critical
   constraints, psychological safety, team culture, role phase, remote or hybrid
   work, or sensory load.
6. Identify the strongest role-context hypothesis.
7. Complete the role-design output before the joint conversation.

Useful role levers include:

- Add one bounded complexity lane.
- Define decision zones: person decides, discuss together, organization decides.
- Create a 30-day learning sprint.
- Use a structured quality dialogue: issue, evidence, risk, trade-off, decision,
  owner, review moment.
- Use a one-page translation format: observation, implication, recommendation,
  risk of inaction.
- Protect focus time or reduce unnecessary meeting load.

The employer-side output should answer:

- Which contribution is currently strongest?
- Which capacity may be hidden, blocked, underused, overused, or misunderstood?
- Which dimension is the best 30-day experiment priority?
- What can the adult reasonably adjust?
- What can the manager or employer reasonably adjust?
- What cannot be changed because of legitimate constraints?
- Which constraints may be avoidable process noise?
- What evidence would show improvement?

## 8. Joint Conversation Flow

Use `docs/WORKBOOK_v1.md` for the shared experiment sections and
`docs/MANAGER_CONVERSATION_GUIDE_v1.md` for the meeting flow.

Recommended participants:

- Adult.
- Manager or employer-side decision maker.
- HR partner, coach, or facilitator when the situation is sensitive, complex, or
  at risk of misunderstanding.

Preparation:

1. Confirm whether the Adult Self-Scan and Employer Role Scan have been
   completed or partially completed.
2. Confirm which examples are safe to discuss.
3. Confirm which private topics are excluded.
4. Decide whether facilitation is needed.
5. Re-state the boundary: this is a role-design conversation, not a diagnosis,
   performance judgment, promotion case, or ranking process.

Conversation sequence:

1. Start with what is working.
2. Name the strongest useful contribution.
3. Identify hidden, blocked, underused, overused, or misunderstood capacity.
4. Compare adult and employer signals across the six fit dimensions.
5. Discuss context conditions.
6. Choose one pathway hypothesis for now.
7. Select adult adjustment options.
8. Select manager or employer adjustment options.
9. Define one 30-day work experiment.
10. Set a review date and stop conditions.

Keep the conversation narrow enough to finish with a real agreement. More than
one issue may be present, but the experiment needs focus.

## 9. Designing the 30-Day Experiment

A good experiment is small enough to run, concrete enough to observe, and safe
enough not to become hidden extra workload.

Complete every field in the 30-day experiment table:

- Fit dimension being tested.
- Pathway hypothesis being tested.
- Work hypothesis.
- Specific change for 30 days.
- What the adult will do.
- What the manager or employer will do.
- What will be paused, reduced, or protected to prevent extra overload.
- Legitimate constraints.
- Autonomy zone: person decides.
- Alignment zone: discuss together.
- Organization zone: not open for change now.
- Evidence of improvement.
- Evidence of no improvement or harm.
- Stop conditions.
- Review date.

Good experiment statement:

> For 30 days, we will test whether clearer outcome boundaries plus more method
> freedom on a specific workstream increases useful contribution without
> increasing workload.

Weak experiment statement:

> We will see if this person is actually gifted or high potential.

The second version is out of scope because it turns role design into identity
proof or ranking.

## 10. Review After 30 Days

Use the review section of the Joint Conversation Canvas.

Review:

- What changed in contribution, clarity, energy, or collaboration?
- Which evidence supports that?
- What did not improve?
- Did the experiment create hidden workload or new risk?
- What did the adult learn?
- What did the manager or employer learn about the role context?
- Should the group continue, adjust, stop, or escalate?

Choose one next decision:

- Continue the experiment for another 30 days.
- Adjust and run a new experiment.
- Convert the experiment into a role agreement.
- Escalate to HR, coach, occupational health, or another specialist route.
- Conclude that the role cannot be redesigned enough.

Do not turn the review into a retroactive performance judgment. Review the
experiment and role context, not the person's worth.

## 11. Privacy and Disclosure

Completed scans may contain sensitive personal, workplace, health-adjacent,
conflict, or HR information. Treat them accordingly.

Minimum privacy practice:

- The adult decides what personal information is private or shared.
- Do not require disclosure of giftedness, neurodivergence, health, burnout,
  conflict, or exit-risk information to use the tool.
- Share only what is necessary for the role-design conversation.
- Do not store completed scans in HR systems without clear purpose, consent,
  retention, and access rules.
- Coaches and facilitators should clarify how notes, exports, and client
  records will be handled.
- Marketing, feedback, and website forms should not request completed scans or
  identifiable workplace conflict details.

If privacy cannot be protected, use the workbook privately and do not run the
shared conversation until conditions improve.

## 12. When to Pause or Escalate

Pause ordinary use and seek appropriate support when:

- The situation is disciplinary, adversarial, legally sensitive, or unsafe.
- Health, burnout, disability, occupational-health, or formal accommodation
  questions dominate.
- The employer wants to use the tool for performance, selection, promotion,
  ranking, or support entitlement.
- The adult feels pressured to disclose private information.
- There is sharp disagreement about evidence and no facilitator is present.
- The role cannot realistically be changed, but the conversation keeps
  pressuring the adult to adapt.
- The experiment would add workload without reducing, pausing, or protecting
  something else.

Possible escalation routes include HR, a coach, a facilitator, occupational
health, legal advice, a formal accommodation process, clinical support, or a
role/career decision process. Choose the route based on the actual issue. The
tool itself does not make those determinations.

## 13. Practical Facilitation Notes

For a 60- to 90-minute session:

| Time | Focus |
|---:|---|
| 5-10 min | Re-state purpose, boundaries, privacy, and agenda |
| 10-15 min | What is working and strongest contribution |
| 15-20 min | Compare the six fit dimensions and evidence |
| 10-15 min | Select one pathway hypothesis and one fit dimension |
| 15-25 min | Design the 30-day experiment |
| 5-10 min | Confirm stop conditions, review date, and what will be documented |

Facilitator interventions:

- Bring abstract labels back to concrete work examples.
- Stop scoring or ranking language.
- Protect the adult's disclosure boundary.
- Ask the employer side to name role/context levers.
- Ask the adult side to name translation, boundary, timing, or request changes
  they can reasonably test.
- Keep the output to one experiment.
- Pause if the conversation becomes a diagnosis, performance review, legal
  dispute, health determination, or support-entitlement decision.

## 14. Current Limits

The current package is draft material.

Not yet present:

- Facilitator guide as a separate artifact.
- 30-day experiment tracker outside the joint canvas.
- Sample report.
- Final terms of use, final privacy notice, license, or legal review.
- PDF, DOCX, or print-verified editions.
- Automated content QA.
- Evidence from user testing or validation.

Do not claim that the tool is validated, diagnostic, predictive, normed,
clinically reviewed, legally reviewed, or ready for HR decision use.

## 15. Quick Start

For an individual adult:

1. Open `docs/WORKBOOK_v1.md`.
2. Complete only the private sections needed for the current role-fit question.
3. Mark what stays private before sharing anything.
4. Draft one minimum useful role-design request and optional manager-safe
   summary.

For a manager or employer:

1. Open `docs/MANAGER_CONVERSATION_GUIDE_v1.md`.
2. Review the boundaries and choose the 30-minute or 60-minute path.
3. Prepare role/context notes using concrete examples, not personal labels.
4. Identify one adjustable role lever and one reviewable experiment candidate.

For a facilitated pair or trio:

1. Adult prepares privately.
2. Manager or employer prepares from the manager conversation guide.
3. Open the shared experiment sections of `docs/WORKBOOK_v1.md`.
4. Agree on one fit dimension, one hypothesis, and one 30-day experiment.
5. Review after 30 days and decide whether to continue, adjust, formalize,
   escalate, or stop.
