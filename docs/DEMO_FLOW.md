# Demo Flow

This demo shows how the **High-Complexity Contribution Tool** / **Instrument
Complexiteit naar Bijdrage** can move from private reflection to a bounded
role-design experiment.

The demo is fictional. It is not a diagnosis, assessment, validation study,
performance review, manager decision process, or advice for a real workplace
case.

## 1. Purpose

Use this demo to understand the tool without completing the full workbook.

It is designed for:

- A new adult user who wants to see the flow before using private prompts.
- A coach, advisor, HR partner, or facilitator reviewing whether the material
  is usable.
- A manager or employer participant learning the responsible-use boundaries.
- A beta reviewer checking clarity, safety, and product fit.

The demo shows a realistic but synthetic path through selected workbook
responses, interpretation, a manager-safe summary, a role-design conversation,
and one 30-day experiment.

The demo does not ask viewers to submit personal information, complete a real
scan, make employment decisions, or share sensitive workplace details.

## 2. Demo Scenario Overview

The scenario uses a fictional adult professional in a knowledge-work role.
There are no real names, real employers, real clients, or identifiable details.

| Scenario element | Demo detail |
|---|---|
| Adult professional | A senior product operations analyst in a mid-sized software services context. |
| Current role context | The role combines client delivery coordination, process improvement, reporting, and recurring escalation support. |
| What is working | The adult quickly detects system patterns, spots repeat failure points, and creates useful cross-team explanations. |
| Friction or underuse pattern | The adult is often pulled into routine rescue work and meeting-heavy coordination, while higher-value analysis is requested late or not at all. |
| Manager or employer context | The manager values reliability and client responsiveness but worries that deeper analysis could slow delivery or create too many parallel changes. |
| Why the tool is relevant | The situation is not suited to diagnosis, proof, ranking, or performance framing. It is suited to a role-design conversation about complexity, autonomy, learning, translation, energy, and workload protection. |

Core demo hypothesis:

> If one bounded complexity lane is protected and routine escalation work is
> limited to agreed windows, useful contribution may increase without adding
> hidden workload.

## 3. What the Demo Shows

The demo shows these parts of the current package:

- Individual reflection: selected private workbook answers grounded in work
  examples.
- Pattern interpretation: signals, alternative explanations, and what not to
  conclude.
- Manager-safe summary: what the adult may choose to share and what remains
  private.
- Role-design conversation: a bounded manager conversation using work-language.
- 30-day experiment: one specific change, owner actions, boundaries, review
  evidence, and stop conditions.
- Review criteria: what to check after 30 days without turning the review into
  a performance rating.

## 4. What the Demo Does Not Show

The demo does not show:

- Diagnosis.
- IQ testing, giftedness proof, psychometric scoring, or validation.
- Performance rating.
- Hiring, promotion, selection, high-potential, or capability suitability.
- A full assessment.
- Mental health, legal, occupational-health, disability, or formal HR advice.
- Employer decision-making about the adult.
- Automated interpretation, dashboards, analytics, accounts, data storage, or
  form submission.
- A guarantee that the experiment will improve performance, retention, health,
  engagement, or conflict.

If a real situation needs legal, clinical, occupational-health, formal HR,
disciplinary, accommodation, or crisis support, this demo is not the right
route.

## 5. Demo Path

### Step 1: Read Scenario

Read the scenario overview above and the fictional setup in
[`../examples/example_adult_workflow.md`](../examples/example_adult_workflow.md).

Look for work-context signals, not traits to label.

### Step 2: Review Selected Workbook Responses

Open the selected responses in
[`../examples/example_adult_workflow.md`](../examples/example_adult_workflow.md).

Focus on:

- Cognitive complexity.
- Learning speed.
- Autonomy and trust.
- Values sensitivity.
- Intensity and energy cost.
- Role clarity.
- Social and communication fit.
- Work-context blockers.
- Sustainable contribution conditions.

Do not treat the responses as a score, profile, or complete assessment.

### Step 3: Identify Patterns and Alternative Explanations

Use the pattern interpretation section in the adult workflow example.

Ask:

- Which signals repeat across work situations?
- Which signals might be explained by workload, timing, role ambiguity,
  communication format, or manager constraints?
- Which conclusion would be too strong?
- Which small role-design hypothesis is worth testing first?

### Step 4: Prepare Manager-Safe Summary

Review the manager-safe summary in the adult workflow example.

Check that it:

- Uses work examples rather than identity proof.
- Names one adjustable role condition.
- Keeps private material private.
- Proposes one bounded 30-day experiment.
- Avoids diagnosis, performance, ranking, or entitlement language.

### Step 5: Run Conversation Using Manager Guide

Open
[`../examples/example_manager_conversation.md`](../examples/example_manager_conversation.md)
alongside
[`MANAGER_CONVERSATION_GUIDE_v1.md`](MANAGER_CONVERSATION_GUIDE_v1.md).

Use either the 30-minute or 60-minute version.

The manager should ask about role/context conditions, not private labels,
health details, IQ, or proof of giftedness.

### Step 6: Define Role-Design Experiment

Open
[`../examples/example_30_day_experiment.md`](../examples/example_30_day_experiment.md).

Check that the experiment includes:

- Fit dimension being tested.
- Work-context hypothesis.
- Adult action.
- Manager action.
- Workload protection.
- Legitimate constraints.
- Evidence of improvement and harm.
- Stop conditions.
- Review date.

### Step 7: Review After 30 Days

Use the final review section in the 30-day experiment example.

The review should ask what happened in the experiment, not whether the adult is
gifted, high-potential, underperforming, or entitled to a permanent change.

The next decision should be one of:

- Continue.
- Adjust.
- Try a different experiment.
- Escalate to a broader role conversation.
- Pause.

## 9. Facilitator Notes

Use the demo as a walkthrough, not as a personal disclosure exercise.

For a coach or facilitator:

- Ask whether the example gives enough structure for a real session.
- Notice where an adult might over-disclose.
- Notice where a manager might drift toward performance framing.
- Ask what script, worksheet, or handout would still be missing.

For a manager or HR participant:

- Ask which manager questions feel safe and useful.
- Ask what the manager should document and what should stay out of records.
- Ask whether the role-design lever is realistic within constraints.
- Watch for language that sounds like support entitlement, capability
  judgment, or employee assessment.

For an employer pilot sponsor:

- Ask whether voluntary participation and no-adverse-consequence boundaries are
  clear enough.
- Ask what written scope would be needed before any real pilot.
- Ask how private workbook material would be kept out of HR files.
- Watch for pressure to standardize too early.

For a beta reviewer:

- Ask whether the demo can be understood in 10 minutes.
- Ask whether the artifact links are clear.
- Ask whether the example is concrete without becoming too personal.
- Watch for any wording that implies validation, diagnosis, performance
  improvement, or legal/clinical advice.

Useful questions to ask during facilitation:

- What is the smallest useful role-design hypothesis here?
- What alternative explanation should we keep visible?
- What should the adult not have to share?
- What would make the experiment fair to both adult and manager?
- What would cause us to stop or pause the experiment?

Reactions to watch for:

- Relief that the conversation can stay practical.
- Concern that the example still feels too personal for work.
- Manager desire for a clearer operational template.
- HR concern about documentation boundaries.
- Coach concern about facilitation scripts.
- Any pull toward scoring, proof, diagnosis, performance, or ranking.

## 10. Demo Review Questions

Use these questions after a quick or full demo:

- What became clearer about the tool's purpose?
- What still felt vague, abstract, or too broad?
- Which artifact was most useful?
- Which artifact would you open first in real use?
- Which language felt safe enough for a manager conversation?
- Which language felt unsafe, diagnostic, identity-heavy, HR-coded, or too
  close to performance review?
- Did the adult's disclosure control remain clear?
- Did the manager-side documentation boundary remain clear?
- Did the experiment feel small enough to test in 30 days?
- What would need to exist before real use?
- What would need to exist before employer-supported use?
- What should remain out of scope?

Record demo feedback as product learning only. Do not collect completed
workbook responses, sensitive personal details, real employer names, conflict
stories, HR case facts, or health-adjacent information.
