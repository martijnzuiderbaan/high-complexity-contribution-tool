# Workbook v1

This workbook helps a gifted, hoogbegaafde, or high-complexity adult and their
work context prepare a practical conversation about sustainable contribution.

The central question is:

> Under what work conditions does complex capacity become useful, sustainable
> contribution?

Use the workbook to turn observations into role-design hypotheses and one
bounded 30-day experiment. Do not use it to prove giftedness, diagnose a person,
rank talent, decide employment outcomes, or make health, legal,
occupational-health, disability, hiring, promotion, or performance decisions.

## Purpose and Boundaries

The workbook supports reflection, preparation, and a shared conversation about
person-role-context fit. It is useful when work tension seems connected to
complexity, autonomy, learning speed, values, quality, intensity, sparring,
translation, energy, role clarity, or boundaries.

It can help users:

- Name concrete work patterns without reducing the adult to a label.
- Separate person patterns from role and context patterns.
- Identify where contribution is strong, hidden, blocked, overused, or
  misunderstood.
- Prepare a safer manager, HR, coaching, or facilitated conversation.
- Define one 30-day work experiment with evidence, workload protection, stop
  conditions, and a review date.

It cannot conclude:

- Whether someone is gifted, hoogbegaafd, neurodivergent, burned out, disabled,
  clinically unwell, high-potential, underperforming, or entitled to support.
- Whether a person should be hired, promoted, selected, ranked, disciplined, or
  formally accommodated.
- Whether a conflict, health issue, legal issue, or occupational-health issue
  is present.

If those questions are central, pause this workbook and involve the appropriate
professional route.

## Who Should Complete It

Adult:

- Complete the private sections first.
- Choose what to keep private and what may be shared.
- Bring only concrete examples and requests that are safe and useful.

Manager or employer:

- Use the joint sections to understand work conditions, not to judge the person.
- Look for role levers, constraints, and avoidable friction.
- Avoid treating workbook content as performance evidence or an HR assessment.

Coach, HR partner, or facilitator:

- Help both sides stay concrete, non-diagnostic, and role-design oriented.
- Protect disclosure boundaries and prevent over-interpretation.
- Pause use if the situation is unsafe, disciplinary, legally sensitive, or
  dominated by health, disability, accommodation, or occupational-health
  questions.

## How Long It Takes

Suggested timing:

| Use | Typical time | Output |
|---|---:|---|
| Private adult scan | 45-75 min | Personal pattern notes and disclosure choices |
| Manager preparation | 30-45 min | Role/context observations and possible levers |
| Facilitated preparation | 20-30 min per participant | Safe examples and agenda focus |
| Joint conversation | 60 min | One 30-day experiment |
| 30-day review | 30 min | Continue, adjust, formalize, escalate, or stop |

Partial completion is acceptable. It is better to bring two concrete work
examples than to complete every field abstractly.

## How to Use It Alone

Use this workbook alone when you want clarity before a coaching, manager, HR,
career, or role decision conversation.

1. Complete the current work situation.
2. Mark the strongest contribution pattern and the largest fit gap.
3. Choose two or three concrete work examples.
4. Decide what you will keep private.
5. Draft a minimum useful request.
6. Sketch one possible 30-day experiment.

Do not pressure yourself to share the completed workbook. You can share only a
summary, a request, or nothing at all.

## How to Use It Before a Manager Conversation

Use this workbook to prepare work-language, not identity proof.

Bring:

- One example of strong contribution.
- One example of blocked, underused, overused, or misunderstood capacity.
- One request framed as a role-design experiment.
- One boundary about what you are not asking for.
- One suggestion for evidence after 30 days.

Avoid bringing:

- A demand that the manager accept a label.
- Private health, conflict, or identity information that you do not want stored
  or repeated.
- A broad redesign request without a small testable starting point.

## How to Use It in a Facilitated Session

Before the session:

- Adult completes private sections and chooses shareable content.
- Manager or employer prepares role/context observations.
- Facilitator checks whether the situation is safe and in scope.
- Everyone agrees that the session is not a diagnosis, performance review,
  promotion case, selection process, or support-entitlement decision.

During the session:

1. Start with what is working.
2. Compare concrete examples.
3. Select one fit dimension that matters most now.
4. Convert the pattern into a role-design hypothesis.
5. Define one 30-day experiment.
6. Agree what will be documented, who sees it, and when it will be reviewed.

## Safety and Misuse Boundaries

Stop or slow down if:

- The adult feels pressured to disclose private information.
- The employer wants to use the workbook for scoring, ranking, selection,
  promotion, performance review, discipline, or support entitlement.
- The conversation becomes a debate about whether someone is really gifted.
- Health, burnout, disability, legal, occupational-health, or formal
  accommodation questions dominate.
- The situation is adversarial, unsafe, or already disciplinary.
- The proposed experiment adds workload without pausing, reducing, or
  protecting anything else.
- There are no concrete work examples.

The adult controls disclosure. Employer-side use must remain focused on the
role and context, not on ranking or diagnosing the person.

## Working Language

Use these terms consistently:

| Term | Meaning in this workbook |
|---|---|
| Signal | An observation that may be relevant, not proof. |
| Pattern | A repeated signal across situations or time. |
| Hypothesis | A possible explanation to test in work terms. |
| Work-context fit | The match between adult, role, task, team, manager, and constraints. |
| Role-design lever | A practical change in work, decision rights, support, focus, or communication. |
| 30-day experiment | A small, time-bound work change with evidence and review. |

Avoid using the workbook as a scorecard. Empty fields and uncertainty are valid
outputs.

## 3. Cognitive Complexity

Core question:

> Is the work complex, systemic, ambiguous, and non-routine enough to use the
> adult's thinking without creating unnecessary overdrive?

Reflection prompts:

| Prompt | Notes |
|---|---|
| Where does the work ask for systems thinking, ambiguity handling, pattern recognition, or multi-variable judgment? |  |
| Where is the work too routine, narrow, pre-decided, or mentally idle? |  |
| Where might I add unnecessary complexity myself? |  |
| Which complex contribution is useful to the team, client, mission, or organization? |  |
| Which complexity is not needed right now? |  |

Possible role-design hypothesis:

> If one bounded complexity lane is added or made explicit, then useful
> contribution may increase without expanding the whole role.

## 8. Boredom, Friction, Overload, or Underuse

These are not diagnoses. Treat them as work-context signals.

| Signal | Current example | Possible work-context pattern | What to check before concluding |
|---|---|---|---|
| Boredom or mental idleness |  | Low complexity or low learning | Is recovery, health, motivation, or role phase also relevant? |
| Friction or repeated misunderstanding |  | Weak translation, low trust, low quality dialogue, unclear authority | Is timing, tone, politics, or decision context also relevant? |
| Overload or overdrive |  | Too much responsibility, weak boundaries, excessive meetings, rescue work | Is workload, staffing, health, or perfectionism also relevant? |
| Underuse |  | Capacity not visible, no complexity lane, low recognition | Is the contribution translated into usable work terms? |
