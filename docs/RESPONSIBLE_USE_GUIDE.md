# Responsible Use Guide

This guide defines practical responsible-use rules for the **High-Complexity
Contribution Tool** / **Instrument Complexiteit naar Bijdrage**.

The tool supports structured reflection and role-design conversations. It does
not diagnose, score, assess, rank, select, or decide anything about a person.

## 1. Purpose

The purpose of this guide is to help adults, coaches, managers, HR partners,
facilitators, and employers use the workbook safely and within scope.

It is intended for:

- Individual reflection.
- Coaching or advisory conversations.
- Preparation for manager conversations.
- Facilitated role-design conversations.
- Carefully bounded employer pilots.
- Workshops where the boundaries are explicit.

It is not a legal, clinical, occupational-health, data-protection, or HR policy
document. It should be reviewed before public launch, paid use, or employer
deployment.

## 2. Core Principles

Responsible use depends on these principles:

- Voluntary participation: no adult should be required to complete or disclose
  private workbook material.
- Adult agency: the adult decides what stays private and what may be shared.
- Work-context fit over person judgment: the conversation focuses on role
  conditions, contribution, learning, autonomy, quality dialogue, sparring,
  translation, energy, and boundaries.
- Patterns and hypotheses, not labels: outputs are working hypotheses for
  discussion, not conclusions about identity, ability, health, or performance.
- Role design over performance rating: the goal is one bounded work experiment,
  not a judgment about past performance.
- Minimum necessary information: collect or share only what is needed for the
  conversation or experiment.
- No selection use: the tool must not be used for hiring, promotion,
  high-potential ranking, screening, succession, or capability comparison.
- Human review and context: interpretation stays qualitative, contextual, and
  human-led.

## 3. Allowed Uses

Allowed uses include:

- Individual reflection by a gifted, hoogbegaafde, or high-complexity adult.
- Coaching, career counseling, advisory, or facilitation conversations.
- Preparation for a manager conversation.
- Joint role-design conversation between an adult and manager.
- HR or people-development support where the adult participates voluntarily.
- Bounded employer pilot with clear scope, data handling, and exit option.
- Workshop use where examples are synthetic or anonymized and participants are
  not required to disclose private material.

Allowed use should end in practical next steps such as:

- A manager-safe summary.
- One fit dimension to explore.
- One role-design hypothesis.
- One 30-day experiment.
- Review evidence and stop conditions.
- A decision to continue, adjust, formalize, escalate, or stop.

## 4. Disallowed Uses

Do not use the tool for:

- Hiring, screening, candidate comparison, or selection.
- Promotion, succession, high-potential ranking, or capability ranking.
- Firing, discipline, performance rating, or performance management.
- Diagnosis, medical screening, psychological screening, clinical advice, or
  treatment planning.
- Occupational-health determinations, disability determinations, formal
  accommodation decisions, or legal decisions.
- Employee surveillance, automated analysis, or dashboards built from completed
  personal responses.
- Mandatory disclosure of giftedness, neurodivergence, health status, conflict
  history, or private workbook content.
- Employer-only evaluation of an adult without voluntary adult participation.

If a requested use needs a score, category, ranking, eligibility decision, or
employment decision, it is outside the scope of this tool.

## 5. Adult-Facing Responsible Use

Adults can use the workbook safely by keeping private reflection separate from
manager-facing conversation.

Use the workbook to:

- Name concrete work examples.
- Notice patterns across complexity, autonomy, learning, quality dialogue,
  sparring, translation, energy, and boundaries.
- Decide which examples are safe and useful to share.
- Prepare a short role-design request.
- Ask for one bounded 30-day experiment rather than a broad role redesign.
- Define what evidence would make the experiment worth continuing.

Before sharing with an employer, decide:

- What is useful for the work conversation.
- What is personal and should remain private.
- What could be misunderstood or stored inappropriately.
- Whether a coach, facilitator, HR partner, or other support should be present.
- Whether the relationship is safe enough for a direct manager conversation.

Avoid over-identifying with patterns:

- Treat workbook outputs as working observations, not fixed identity.
- Do not use a pattern to prove giftedness or explain everything at work.
- Keep asking what the role/context can change.
- Notice where the issue may be workload, team process, unclear decision rights,
  poor sparring, values friction, or ordinary role mismatch.
- Stop or seek support if the tool starts creating pressure to disclose more
  than feels safe.

## 6. Manager and Employer Responsible Use

Managers and employers should use the tool to listen for role/context
conditions, not to evaluate the adult as a person.

Responsible manager stance:

- Ask for concrete work examples.
- Listen for role levers: complexity lane, decision zone, focus time, learning
  edge, sparring route, quality-dialogue format, translation support, meeting
  load, recovery, and boundaries.
- Separate legitimate constraints from avoidable process noise.
- Ask what small experiment would test the hypothesis safely.
- Agree on review evidence and stop conditions.

Avoid pathologizing or idealizing:

- Do not turn intensity into a defect.
- Do not turn giftedness or high complexity into a special status.
- Do not assume every conflict is caused by the adult's complexity.
- Do not assume every role problem is solved by giving harder work.
- Do not ask for diagnoses, IQ scores, therapy details, or private self-scan
  content.

Avoid turning the tool into a performance conversation:

- Do not use workbook content to rate past performance.
- Do not use patterns to explain poor performance.
- Do not create a personnel-file narrative about the adult's traits.
- Do not ask HR to store completed scans.
- Do not use the tool to decide whether the adult deserves support.

Convert insights into role-design experiments:

1. Name the role/context condition to test.
2. Keep the experiment small and time-bound.
3. Protect workload so the experiment does not add hidden work.
4. Define manager actions as clearly as adult actions.
5. Review observable work evidence after 30 days.

What not to document in HR systems:

- Private self-scan responses.
- Diagnostic or health details.
- Giftedness proof or IQ information.
- Full conflict narratives.
- Personal interpretations about identity, personality, or mental health.
- Third-party personal information.

When documentation is needed, document the agreed role-design action, review
date, operational commitments, and work evidence only.

## 7. Coach and Facilitator Responsible Use

Coaches, advisors, HR partners, and facilitators should protect agency,
privacy, and scope.

Boundaries:

- Present the tool as structured reflection and role-design support.
- Do not sell it as a validated assessment.
- Do not diagnose, score, certify, rank, or prove giftedness.
- Do not imply that the tool can resolve legal, clinical, occupational-health,
  disciplinary, or crisis situations.
- Keep notes and client records limited to what is necessary for the agreed
  service.

Facilitation stance:

- Slow the conversation down when people jump to labels.
- Translate personal experience into work examples and role levers.
- Protect the adult's right not to share private material.
- Keep the manager focused on context and adjustable work conditions.
- Keep the outcome bounded to one experiment where possible.

Handling strong emotion or conflict:

- Normalize pausing, narrowing, or rescheduling.
- Do not force disclosure to complete the worksheet.
- Separate what can be handled through role design from what needs another
  support route.
- Avoid taking sides in a manager-employee dispute.
- Use synthetic or generalized examples in workshops unless participants have
  explicitly chosen to share.

Referral or escalation points:

- Health, crisis, diagnosis, treatment, or therapy needs.
- Occupational-health or disability questions.
- Legal, disciplinary, grievance, harassment, or formal conflict matters.
- Situations where the adult does not feel safe.
- Situations where employer power makes voluntary participation doubtful.

Avoiding overclaiming:

- Say "pattern", "hypothesis", "possible role lever", or "experiment".
- Do not say "validated result", "diagnosis", "profile", "score", "proof", or
  "assessment result".
- Avoid claims about reducing burnout, churn, conflict, or performance problems
  unless there is evidence and the claim is carefully scoped.

## 8. Employer Pilot Rules

Employer pilots require stronger boundaries than individual or coaching use.

Minimum pilot rules:

- Participation is voluntary.
- The adult can decline or pause without adverse consequences.
- The pilot purpose is stated in writing.
- The scope is limited by time, team, participant count, and use case.
- The tool is not used for selection, promotion, performance, discipline,
  ranking, firing, or support entitlement.
- Completed private workbook responses are not stored in HR files.
- The adult controls what private material is shared.
- Data handling is explained before use.
- Outputs focus on role-design actions, review evidence, and stop conditions.
- A facilitator is used when the situation is sensitive or power dynamics are
  likely to distort voluntary participation.
- There is an exit option for the adult and for the pilot sponsor.

Review criteria should include:

- Did participants understand the boundaries?
- Did the tool produce one bounded role-design experiment?
- Did it reduce vague interpretation and increase concrete work examples?
- Did it avoid unnecessary personal disclosure?
- Did any misuse pressure appear?
- What should change before any broader employer use?

## 9. Misuse Scenarios

| Scenario | Risk | Responsible response | Safer alternative |
|---|---|---|---|
| Employer wants to use it in hiring. | The tool becomes a selection or screening instrument and may be treated as a capability test. | Decline this use. Explain that the tool is not for hiring, screening, or candidate comparison. | Use ordinary job-relevant selection methods outside this tool; keep this tool for voluntary role-design conversations with existing adults. |
| Manager wants to use it to explain poor performance. | Workbook patterns may become a personal explanation for performance concerns. | Stop the tool use as a performance frame. Separate performance management from role-design exploration. | If both sides agree voluntarily, use a small role-context experiment focused on work conditions, not blame. |
| Adult wants the tool to prove giftedness. | The workbook becomes identity proof or a substitute for assessment. | Clarify that the tool cannot prove giftedness, IQ, or diagnosis. | Use the workbook to prepare concrete work examples and role-fit requests without requiring proof. |
| Coach wants to sell it as a validated assessment. | Clients may rely on claims the tool does not support. | Require different wording: prototype, reflection support, conversation guide, qualitative hypotheses. | Offer it as a structured workbook or facilitated role-design method with clear boundaries. |
| HR wants to standardize it too early. | A sensitive reflection tool may become a quasi-assessment or HR record. | Pause broad rollout. Require pilot boundaries, voluntary use, privacy handling, and review criteria. | Run a small bounded pilot with synthetic examples first, then voluntary facilitated use. |
| Team conflict is already unsafe. | The tool may intensify conflict or create pressured disclosure. | Do not run an ordinary joint canvas. Pause and use appropriate HR, legal, mediation, occupational-health, or specialist support. | Use private reflection only, or return to the tool later if trust and scope are restored. |

## 10. Responsible-Use Checklist

### Before Use

- Confirm the use is voluntary.
- Confirm the purpose is role-design conversation, not assessment.
- Confirm the tool is not being used for hiring, promotion, performance,
  ranking, diagnosis, surveillance, or employment decisions.
- Confirm the adult controls what private material is shared.
- Confirm sensitive details are not needed for the intended use.
- Decide whether facilitation is needed.
- Decide what will be documented and what will not.

### During Use

- Use concrete work examples.
- Keep private reflection private unless the adult chooses otherwise.
- Translate patterns into role/context hypotheses.
- Avoid labels, scores, and conclusions.
- Keep manager actions and adult actions visible.
- Watch for pressure, blame, over-disclosure, or performance framing.
- Pause if the conversation moves outside scope.

### After Use

- Keep only the minimum notes needed.
- Document agreed role-design actions rather than personal interpretations.
- Review the 30-day experiment using observable evidence.
- Decide continue, adjust, formalize, escalate, or stop.
- Do not reuse participant stories without explicit permission.

### Before Employer Sharing

- Prepare a manager-safe summary.
- Remove private labels, diagnostic details, health details, and full conflict
  narratives.
- Keep the request practical and bounded.
- Confirm who will receive the summary.
- Confirm whether anything will be stored and where.
- Bring a facilitator if the power dynamic or conflict level calls for it.

### Before Paid or Facilitated Use

- Make responsible-use boundaries visible before purchase or session booking.
- Use draft beta disclosure only as draft copy until reviewed.
- Explain data handling and note-taking.
- Avoid validation, diagnostic, clinical, legal, or performance claims.
- Define support limits and escalation routes.
- Keep completed workbook responses out of product-owner systems unless a
  specific reviewed process exists.
