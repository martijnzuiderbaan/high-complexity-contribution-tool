# Manager Conversation Guide v1

This guide helps a manager, employer, HR partner, coach, or facilitator hold a
practical conversation with a gifted, hoogbegaafde, or high-complexity adult
about sustainable contribution.

The goal is not to assess the person. The goal is to understand which role and
context conditions help complex capacity become useful, sustainable work.

## Purpose

Use this guide when there are work-context signals such as:

- Strong capacity that is not fully visible or usable.
- Fast learning followed by boredom, repetition, or underuse.
- Friction around autonomy, decision rights, speed, quality, or values.
- High standards, intensity, or risk sensitivity that may need a better route.
- Overload, overdrive, rescue work, or unsustainable meeting and switching load.
- Repeated misunderstanding between adult, manager, team, or organization.

The intended output is one role-design hypothesis and one 30-day experiment.

## How to Enter the Conversation

Start from respect and work evidence.

Useful opening:

> I would like to understand where your contribution is strongest, where the
> role may be blocking or overusing it, and whether we can test one practical
> work-context adjustment for 30 days.

Set boundaries early:

- This is not a diagnosis.
- This is not an IQ, giftedness, personality, burnout, or health assessment.
- This is not a performance review, promotion case, ranking process, or
  disciplinary meeting.
- The adult controls what personal information is private or shared.
- The manager examines role and context conditions, including legitimate
  constraints.
- The conversation should end with a small experiment, not a full redesign.

If those boundaries cannot be respected, pause and involve the appropriate
support route.

## What the Tool Is and Is Not

| The tool is | The tool is not |
|---|---|
| A structured role-design conversation aid | A psychometric test or diagnostic instrument |
| A way to discuss complex contribution in work terms | A way to prove giftedness or rank talent |
| A way to identify role levers and context blockers | A shortcut for hiring, promotion, selection, or performance decisions |
| A way to agree on one 30-day experiment | A promise of permanent adaptation |
| A way to protect adult agency and privacy | A reason to demand private health, identity, or conflict details |

## Avoid Common Conversation Traps

| Trap | What it sounds like | Better move |
|---|---|---|
| Pathologizing | "Why are you so sensitive or difficult?" | "Which work condition is creating this pattern?" |
| Over-celebrating | "You are gifted, so we should give you everything complex." | "Which specific complexity would create value here?" |
| Minimizing | "Everyone has boring tasks." | "Which routine is necessary, and where is there room for better fit?" |
| Performance framing | "Convince me this improves your performance." | "What evidence would show the role experiment is useful?" |
| Label debate | "Are you really hoogbegaafd?" | "What work examples show underuse, overload, or useful contribution?" |
| Unlimited adaptation | "Tell us everything you need and we will adjust." | "Which bounded change can we test within real constraints?" |
| Blame | "The person or manager is the problem." | "What does the person-role-context pattern suggest?" |

## What to Listen For

Listen for repeated signals, not isolated remarks.

| Listen for | Work-language translation |
|---|---|
| "I learn it once and then it becomes flat." | Learning edge may disappear after fast mastery. |
| "I need to understand why." | Constraints and decision logic may need explanation. |
| "I keep seeing risks no one wants to discuss." | Quality or values dialogue route may be weak. |
| "I am always pulled into fixing things." | Informal rescue work may be overusing capacity. |
| "I cannot get traction for my ideas." | Translation, timing, authority, or sparring may be missing. |
| "I need more freedom." | Outcome boundaries and method freedom may need separation. |
| "I am exhausted but still underused." | The right capacity may be underused while load is too high. |

Do not treat intensity as automatically negative. Ask what work signal may sit
underneath it.

## What to Ask

Ask questions that produce examples and role levers.

- Where is your contribution strongest right now?
- Which work conditions make that possible?
- Where do you feel underused, blocked, overused, or misunderstood?
- Can you give one concrete example?
- Which part is about role design, and which part is about communication or
  timing?
- Which constraints do you understand as legitimate?
- Which constraints feel like avoidable process noise?
- What do you need to keep private?
- What would be a small 30-day experiment, not a full redesign?
- What evidence would show improvement or harm?

## What Not to Ask

Avoid questions that force identity proof, diagnosis, private disclosure, or
employment decision framing.

- Are you officially gifted?
- What is your IQ?
- Do you have a diagnosis?
- Are you burned out?
- Why are you so intense?
- Why can others handle this and you cannot?
- Should this affect your promotion or rating?
- What private health or conflict details explain this?
- Can you prove this is not just preference?
- Do you deserve special treatment?

If a formal HR, health, legal, occupational-health, disability, or accommodation
question is needed, use the right route instead of this workbook conversation.

## Discuss the Main Patterns in Work Terms

## Autonomy and Trust

Useful framing:

> Let us separate outcome clarity, constraints, method freedom, and decision
> rights.

Ask:

- Which outcomes are fixed?
- Which constraints are legitimate?
- Where can method freedom increase without increasing risk?
- Which decisions are yours, shared, or organizational?
- What review rhythm would create alignment without micromanagement?

Possible experiment:

> For 30 days, define decision zones on one workstream and review whether this
> improves speed, quality, clarity, and trust.

## Speed and Learning

Useful framing:

> Fast learning is useful when it connects to a visible next learning edge, not
> only more volume.

Ask:

- Which part of the role was mastered quickly?
- What is still genuinely developmental?
- Where has the role become maintenance or repetition?
- Which new domain, method, stakeholder, or problem would help the work?

Possible experiment:

> For 30 days, attach one learning sprint to real work and define the output,
> review date, and workload boundary.

## Complexity

Useful framing:

> We are looking for the right complexity, not more complexity everywhere.

Ask:

- Which complex problems would create value if scoped well?
- Which routine work is necessary and cannot be removed?
- Where could analysis, system mapping, options work, risk review, or process
  improvement be useful?
- What would be too broad or premature?

Possible experiment:

> For 30 days, add one bounded complexity lane and protect enough time to do it
> properly.

## Intensity and Energy

Useful framing:

> Intensity may carry useful signal, but contribution must remain sustainable.

Ask:

- Which parts of the work create useful intensity?
- Which parts create avoidable drain?
- Where are meetings, switching, rescue work, or social load excessive?
- What needs to be paused, reduced, or protected before adding anything?

Possible experiment:

> For 30 days, protect focus time and pause one informal rescue pattern, then
> review contribution and energy together.

## Friction and Communication

Useful framing:

> Friction may be a signal about timing, translation, authority, trust, values,
> or role design.

Ask:

- What is the concrete work issue underneath the friction?
- Did the concern arrive early enough to be useful?
- Was the concern translated into evidence, risk, and decision language?
- Which forum or timing would work better?

Possible experiment:

> For 30 days, use a one-page format for complex concerns: observation,
> implication, recommendation, risk of inaction, and decision requested.

## Values and Quality

Useful framing:

> Values and quality concerns need a route that separates issue, evidence, risk,
> trade-off, and decision.

Ask:

- What is the quality, ethics, fairness, or risk issue?
- What evidence supports it?
- What trade-off is being made?
- Who owns the decision?
- When is escalation appropriate?

Possible experiment:

> For 30 days, route quality concerns through a structured quality dialogue
> before they become personal conflict.

## Convert Insights Into Role-Design Experiments

Do not try to fix the whole role at once. Choose one testable lever.

| Signal | Possible role-design lever | What to protect |
|---|---|---|
| Low complexity | Bounded complexity lane | Time, scope, and decision relevance |
| Low autonomy | Decision-zone map | Legitimate constraints and review rhythm |
| Low learning | Real-work learning sprint | Workload and output clarity |
| Values friction | Quality-dialogue route | Respectful challenge and decision ownership |
| Weak sparring | Sparring partner or review rhythm | Psychological safety and relevance |
| Translation gap | One-page recommendation format | Feedback on timing and landing |
| Energy overload | Meeting/focus review | Recovery and reduced hidden workload |
| Informal rescue work | Role clarity and stop rules | Recognition and workload boundary |

Experiment quality check:

| Check | Yes/No |
|---|---:|
| The experiment is specific to one workstream or pattern. |  |
| It includes both adult and manager actions. |  |
| It names what will be paused, reduced, or protected. |  |
| It respects legitimate constraints. |  |
| It has evidence of improvement and harm. |  |
| It has stop conditions. |  |
| It has a review date. |  |
| It is not a performance review or diagnosis. |  |

## Agree on Boundaries

Boundary questions:

- What personal information will not be discussed?
- What will be documented?
- Who will see the notes?
- Where will notes be stored?
- What is outside this conversation?
- Which decisions are not being made here?
- What would cause us to stop the experiment early?
- What support route is needed if formal HR, legal, health, disability,
  accommodation, or occupational-health issues emerge?

Document only what is necessary for the role-design experiment. Avoid storing
private workbook content in HR systems unless purpose, consent, access, and
retention are explicit.

Use this structure:

| Element | Agreement |
|---|---|

| Fit dimension |  |
| Role-design hypothesis |  |
| Specific change for 30 days |  |
| Adult action |  |
| Manager or employer action |  |

| Legitimate constraints |  |
| Evidence of improvement |  |
| Evidence of no improvement or harm |  |
| Stop conditions |  |
| Review date |  |
| Documentation boundary |  |

Example:

> For 30 days, we will test whether clearer outcome boundaries plus more method
> freedom on the client-risk review improves decision quality and energy without
> increasing total workload.

## Review Progress

After 30 days, review the experiment directly.

Ask:

- Did the agreed change actually happen?
- What changed in contribution, clarity, energy, or collaboration?
- What evidence supports that?
- What did not improve?
- Did hidden workload appear?
- Did any stop condition occur?
- What did the adult learn?
- What did the manager or employer learn about the role context?
- Should we continue, adjust, formalize, escalate, or stop?

Do not use the review to retroactively rate the person. Review the role
experiment.

## How HR, Coach, or Facilitator Can Support

HR, coach, or facilitator support is useful when:

- The adult is unsure what to disclose.
- Manager and adult have different interpretations.
- Trust is fragile.
- Role constraints are real but poorly understood.
- Quality or values concerns are becoming personal conflict.
- Health, workload, or boundaries may be relevant but should not be diagnosed
  in the session.
- The experiment needs documentation boundaries.

Support tasks:

- Re-state purpose and boundaries.
- Keep the discussion grounded in concrete work examples.
- Stop scoring, ranking, diagnosis, or performance framing.
- Protect privacy and disclosure choice.
- Translate broad concerns into role-design levers.
- Identify when another professional route is needed.

## Suggested 60-Minute Conversation Agenda

| Time | Focus | Output |
|---:|---|---|
| 0-5 min | Purpose, boundaries, privacy, and agenda | Shared frame |
| 5-15 min | What is working and strongest contribution | Contribution statement |
| 15-25 min | Current work-context signals | Two or three examples |
| 25-35 min | Select one fit dimension and hypothesis | Focus for experiment |
| 35-50 min | Design the 30-day experiment | Experiment agreement |
| 50-57 min | Stop conditions, documentation, review date | Safety and follow-up |
| 57-60 min | Confirm next actions | Adult and manager actions |

Facilitator note:

> If the conversation moves into diagnosis, performance, legal, health,
> occupational-health, disability, accommodation, or disciplinary territory,
> pause the agenda and decide whether another route is needed.

## Shorter 30-Minute Version

Use this only when trust is sufficient and preparation is already done.

| Time | Focus | Output |
|---:|---|---|
| 0-5 min | Re-state boundaries and selected examples | Safe frame |
| 5-10 min | Strongest contribution and main fit gap | Shared pattern |
| 10-18 min | Choose one role-design lever | Hypothesis |
| 18-27 min | Define 30-day experiment | Agreement |
| 27-30 min | Review date and stop conditions | Follow-up |

If the 30-minute version feels rushed, stop with preparation only and schedule a
full session.

## Question Bank

## Starting Safely

- What would make this conversation useful and safe enough?
- What should we explicitly not discuss today?
- Which decisions are not being made in this conversation?
- What is one concrete work example we can use?

## Strong Contribution

- Where does your contribution currently have the most value?
- What role conditions make that contribution possible?
- Where has the organization benefited from your way of thinking?
- What should we protect because it already works?

## Complexity and Learning

- Which problems are complex enough to use your thinking well?
- Where is the work too routine or already mastered?
- What is the next useful learning edge?
- What would be a bounded complexity lane, not a full redesign?

## Autonomy and Trust

- Which outcomes are clear?
- Which methods need freedom?
- Which constraints are non-negotiable?
- Which decisions are yours, shared, or organizational?
- What review rhythm would help without becoming control?

## Values and Quality

- What is the concern in work terms?
- What evidence supports it?
- What trade-off is being made?
- What decision is needed, and by whom?
- What would be a better route for raising this earlier?

## Sparring and Communication

- Who can challenge the thinking at the right depth?
- Where does translation break down?
- What format would make complex insight easier to use?
- What feedback would help the message land?

## Energy and Boundaries

- Which load is necessary, and which is avoidable?
- Where is capacity being overused?
- What must be paused, reduced, or protected?
- What would be a realistic stop condition?
