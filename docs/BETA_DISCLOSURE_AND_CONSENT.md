# Beta Disclosure and Consent

This document provides beta disclosure and consent copy for early use of the
**High-Complexity Contribution Tool** / **Instrument Complexiteit naar
Bijdrage**.

It is practical product copy for a private beta. It is not final legal text,
not a complete privacy policy, and not a substitute for legal, clinical,
employment, occupational-health, or data-protection advice.

## 1. Purpose

The purpose of this document is to help the maintainer explain the beta in
plain language before inviting real users, coaches, managers, HR partners, or
employers to use the materials.

It supports:

- Clear disclosure about what the beta is and is not.
- Consent wording for a future beta form, email onboarding, facilitated
  session, or employer-supported pilot.
- Practical boundaries around privacy, disclosure, feedback, and responsible
  use.
- A checklist for the maintainer before real beta intake, employer pilots, or
  paid use.

This copy should be reviewed before public launch, paid use, or employer
deployment. It should not be presented as final legal policy.

## 2. What the Beta Is

The beta is an early prototype of a workbook and facilitation package for
structured role-design conversations.

The beta is:

- An early product prototype.
- Evidence-informed, but not validated as a commercial instrument.
- Structured reflection and conversation support.
- A way to explore person-role-context fit and sustainable contribution.
- A way to prepare one bounded 30-day work experiment.

The beta is not:

- A diagnosis.
- A psychological, medical, clinical, occupational-health, or legal
  assessment.
- A giftedness test, IQ test, psychometric test, or validated assessment.
- A hiring, selection, promotion, high-potential, performance-rating, or
  capability-ranking tool.
- A way to decide whether someone deserves support, accommodations, a role
  change, or employment action.
- An automated interpretation, scoring, ranking, or decision system.

The expected output is a pattern, hypothesis, conversation summary, or
role-design experiment. It is not a conclusion about a person.

## 3. Who the Beta Is For

The beta may be suitable for:

- Gifted, hoogbegaafde, or high-complexity adults exploring work-context fit,
  contribution, underuse, overload, role friction, learning, autonomy, quality
  dialogue, sparring, translation, energy, and boundaries.
- Coaches, advisors, career counselors, HR partners, or facilitators who can
  use the material responsibly and keep the conversation grounded in work
  examples and role design.
- Managers, HR partners, or employers only where the adult participates
  voluntarily, the purpose is clearly bounded, and the use remains focused on
  role/context design rather than person judgment.

Employer-supported use should be especially careful. The adult must understand
the purpose, control what personal material is shared, and be able to decline
or pause participation without adverse consequences.

## 4. Who Should Not Use the Beta

Do not use the beta for:

- Hiring, selection, screening, or candidate comparison.
- Promotion, succession, high-potential ranking, or talent ranking.
- Performance management, disciplinary processes, firing decisions, or
  capability judgments.
- Employer-only evaluation of an employee without the adult's voluntary
  participation.
- Clinical assessment, mental-health assessment, diagnosis, crisis support, or
  treatment planning.
- Occupational-health, disability, accommodation, legal, or formal employment
  determinations.
- Conflict escalation where there is not enough trust to hold a bounded
  role-design conversation.
- Situations where the real need is legal, medical, occupational-health,
  therapeutic, or formal HR support.

If a user or buyer wants a test, proof of giftedness, ranking system, or
employee decision tool, this beta is not the right product.

## 5. What Participants Should Understand Before Using It

Before using the beta, participants should understand that:

- The tool produces patterns and hypotheses, not conclusions.
- Participation should be voluntary.
- The adult controls what is shared with an employer.
- Private reflection can remain private.
- Sensitive personal details should not be entered, sent, or shared unless they
  are explicitly needed, safe to share, and handled through an appropriate
  process.
- The focus is work-context design: role conditions, contribution, learning,
  autonomy, quality dialogue, sparring, translation, energy, boundaries, and a
  bounded experiment.
- A manager-safe summary should use work examples and role-design requests, not
  personal labels, diagnostic information, or full conflict narratives.
- The beta should not replace professional support when the situation involves
  health, legal, occupational-health, crisis, disciplinary, or serious conflict
  issues.

Participants should be able to stop, pause, or narrow the conversation if the
use becomes unsafe, too personal, or too close to an employment decision.

## 6. Data and Privacy Expectations for the Current Static Prototype

The current static website form does not submit or store data.

Current prototype posture:

- The website is static HTML, CSS, and JavaScript.
- The beta form is front-end only.
- The form does not send email, write to a database, connect to a mailing list,
  or create a backend record.
- The Markdown files are local/static documents.
- There are no accounts, analytics providers, payment providers, backend APIs,
  or database storage in the current repository.

Any real beta intake must be handled manually until a real privacy-reviewed
process exists.

For the current beta stage, avoid collecting:

- Sensitive health, diagnosis, disability, burnout, treatment, or
  occupational-health information.
- Full employment-conflict narratives.
- HR case details, performance records, disciplinary details, or promotion
  decisions.
- Third-party personal information about managers, colleagues, employees,
  clients, or candidates.
- Completed workbook responses unless a specific, safe, reviewed process exists
  for that purpose.

Beta intake should ask only for the minimum information needed to decide
whether the use case fits the private beta and how to follow up responsibly.

## 7. Suggested Beta Consent Text

These examples are draft product copy. They should be reviewed before public
deployment, paid use, or employer pilots.

### Short Website Checkbox Version

> I understand this is an early prototype for structured reflection and
> role-design conversation support. It is not a diagnosis, assessment, hiring
> tool, performance tool, or clinical advice. I will not submit completed
> scans, health details, employment-conflict details, HR records, or
> third-party personal information through this beta request.

### Longer Email or Onboarding Version

> Before receiving beta materials, please confirm that you understand the
> current tool is an early workbook prototype. It is evidence-informed but not
> validated as a commercial assessment. It is intended to support private
> reflection, coaching, or bounded role-design conversations. It must not be
> used for diagnosis, giftedness proof, hiring, promotion, performance rating,
> ranking, employee surveillance, clinical advice, occupational-health
> decisions, legal decisions, or employer-only evaluation.
>
> Please do not send completed workbook responses, sensitive health details,
> diagnostic information, full conflict narratives, HR case material, or
> identifiable information about other people. If you share beta feedback,
> keep it about clarity, usefulness, missing guidance, confusing wording, and
> responsible-use fit. You may ask for your beta contact details or feedback
> notes to be deleted from the maintainer's records.

### Facilitated Session Version

> This session uses an early workbook prototype to structure a role-design
> conversation. The tool does not diagnose, score, assess, rank, or decide
> anything about you. You decide what personal material stays private and what
> is useful to bring into the session. We will focus on concrete work examples,
> role/context conditions, and one bounded 30-day experiment. If the
> conversation moves into health, legal, disciplinary, crisis, or formal HR
> territory, we will pause and consider a more appropriate support route.

### Employer-Supported Use Version

> This employer-supported use is voluntary and bounded. The purpose is to
> explore role/context conditions and possible role-design experiments, not to
> assess, rank, diagnose, rate performance, decide promotion, or determine
> employment outcomes. The adult controls what private material is shared. The
> employer should not request or store completed private workbook responses in
> an HR file. Any shared output should be limited to practical role-design
> summaries, agreed actions, review evidence, and stop conditions.

## 8. User Control and Disclosure

### What the Adult May Choose to Share

The adult may choose to share:

- A short description of the role-fit question.
- One or two concrete work examples.
- The fit dimension that seems most relevant now.
- A practical role-design request.
- A proposed 30-day experiment.
- Evidence that would make the experiment useful or not useful.
- Boundaries around workload, focus time, meeting load, quality dialogue, or
  recovery.

The adult does not need to share labels, diagnoses, test results, medical
information, family history, therapy history, full conflict narratives, or
private workbook notes to use the tool.

### What Should Remain Private

Keep private unless there is a clear, safe, and appropriate reason to share:

- Completed private self-scan responses.
- Diagnostic labels, IQ scores, giftedness proof, health information, burnout
  details, disability details, treatment history, or occupational-health
  material.
- Personal conflict narratives that are not needed for role design.
- Information about colleagues, managers, clients, or third parties.
- Anything the adult would not want stored, forwarded, or interpreted outside
  the conversation.

### How to Prepare a Manager-Safe Summary

A manager-safe summary should be concise and work-oriented:

1. Name the work situation without personal labels.
2. Give one concrete example of useful contribution or blocked contribution.
3. Name the role/context condition that may be adjustable.
4. Ask for one bounded experiment.
5. Define review evidence and stop conditions.

Example structure:

> In the last month, I have seen repeated rework in [work area]. I think the
> main adjustable condition is [complexity lane / decision zone / focus time /
> quality-dialogue route / sparring format]. I would like to test [one
> bounded experiment] for 30 days and review [evidence] on [date].

### How to Avoid Turning the Workbook Into an HR File

For employer-supported use:

- Do not ask the adult to submit the private self-scan.
- Do not store full workbook responses in HR systems.
- Do not copy sensitive personal, health, diagnostic, conflict, or third-party
  details into personnel files.
- Document only the agreed role-design action, review date, evidence, and
  operational commitments when documentation is needed.
- Keep reflection notes separate from employment decisions.
- Use ordinary manager notes only for agreed work changes, not personal
  interpretation.

## 9. Feedback Participation

Beta feedback should help improve the product without collecting sensitive case
material.

Feedback may ask about:

- Which sections were clear, useful, too long, or confusing.
- Whether the workflow helped users prepare a safer conversation.
- Whether the boundaries were visible and practical.
- Whether prompts produced concrete examples and role-design options.
- What was missing for adults, coaches, managers, HR partners, or employers.
- Whether pricing, packaging, or facilitation assumptions seem realistic.

Feedback should avoid:

- Completed workbook responses.
- Diagnostic, health, treatment, burnout, disability, or occupational-health
  details.
- Full conflict narratives or HR case histories.
- Identifiable stories about other employees, managers, colleagues, clients, or
  candidates.
- Performance records, disciplinary records, promotion decisions, or legal
  facts.

Quotes or testimonials should be handled carefully:

- Use a quote only with explicit permission.
- Confirm the exact wording before publishing.
- Allow the person to choose whether their name, role, organization, or a
  generic descriptor is used.
- Do not publish quotes that reveal sensitive health, diagnosis, conflict, HR,
  or third-party information.
- Keep quote permission separate from beta participation.

Anonymization should be conservative:

- Remove names, organizations, job titles, locations, client names, and dates
  when they are not needed.
- Generalize small or recognizable contexts.
- Avoid using a story if the person or workplace could still be recognized.
- Prefer aggregate product-learning notes over detailed personal stories.

## 10. Red Flags and Stop Conditions

Do not continue ordinary beta use when:

- The adult feels pressured to participate or disclose personal material.
- The employer wants to use the tool for hiring, promotion, performance,
  ranking, discipline, firing, support entitlement, or proof of capability.
- The main issue is health, crisis, legal action, occupational health,
  disability determination, formal accommodation, or disciplinary process.
- The relationship is too unsafe or adversarial for a bounded role-design
  conversation.
- A participant asks for diagnosis, giftedness proof, a score, or a validated
  assessment conclusion.
- The conversation is becoming a debate about the adult as a person rather than
  work/context conditions.
- The proposed 30-day experiment would add hidden workload, remove necessary
  support, or create foreseeable harm.

Pause a manager conversation when:

- The adult is no longer comfortable with the material being discussed.
- The manager asks for private self-scan content.
- The discussion shifts into blame, performance judgment, or disciplinary
  language.
- Either side cannot agree on a small, observable, bounded experiment.
- The next responsible step is specialist advice rather than more workbook
  discussion.

Consider involving a coach, HR partner, legal advisor, occupational-health
professional, therapist, medical professional, union or works-council route, or
other appropriate support when the situation requires expertise outside a
role-design conversation.

This is a conservative boundary, not a crisis label. It simply means the beta
tool should not be used as the primary support in situations it was not built
to handle.

## 11. Maintainer Checklist

### Before Real Beta Intake Starts

- Confirm the beta is still manual and not collecting data through the static
  site.
- Publish or send this beta disclosure and consent copy.
- Publish or send the privacy placeholder.
- Publish or send the responsible-use guide.
- Define the minimum beta intake fields.
- Remove or avoid free-text prompts that invite sensitive disclosure.
- Define where beta contact details and feedback notes will be stored.
- Define who can access beta records.
- Define a deletion route for beta contact details and feedback notes.
- Prepare a response for unsuitable use requests.
- Prepare a response for accidental sensitive disclosure.

### Before Employer Pilots

- Require voluntary adult participation.
- Confirm the adult controls what private material is shared.
- Confirm the employer will not use the tool for hiring, promotion,
  performance, ranking, discipline, firing, support entitlement, or employee
  evaluation.
- Confirm completed private workbook responses will not be stored in HR files.
- Define the pilot scope, participant count, timing, purpose, and exit option.
- Define what may be documented: role-design actions, review dates, evidence,
  and operational commitments.
- Define who facilitates, who receives outputs, and who can stop the process.
- Prepare escalation routes for health, legal, occupational-health, formal HR,
  or conflict issues.

### Before Paid Use

- Replace draft consent and privacy copy with reviewed public materials.
- Add terms, license scope, refund/support boundaries, and payment disclosures.
- Decide whether the first paid offer is individual, professional, employer
  pilot, or workshop-led.
- Confirm the delivery model does not require accounts unless there is a
  proven operational need.
- Review payment, invoice, tax, access, and customer-support handling.
- Make responsible-use boundaries visible before purchase.
- Avoid claims of validation, diagnosis, prediction, performance improvement,
  churn reduction, burnout prevention, or legal/clinical usefulness unless
  there is reviewed evidence and appropriate scope.
- Keep beta feedback and completed workbook content out of the sales funnel.
