# Interpretation Guide v1

This guide helps adults, coaches, HR partners, facilitators, and managers
interpret workbook patterns responsibly.

Interpretation means:

> Look for repeated work-context signals, form a practical role-design
> hypothesis, and test it carefully.

Interpretation does not mean:

> Diagnose the person, score capacity, rank talent, prove giftedness, decide
> support entitlement, or make employment, health, legal, or
> occupational-health decisions.

## Purpose and Boundaries

Use this guide after reviewing the workbook, adult preparation, employer role
scan, or joint conversation notes.

The guide can help users:

- Notice repeated signals across concrete work examples.
- Separate adult-side patterns from context-side patterns.
- Identify contribution blockers.
- Select usable role-design hypotheses.
- Prevent over-interpretation.
- Decide when to continue, slow down, facilitate, or refer elsewhere.

The guide cannot conclude:

- Whether someone is gifted, hoogbegaafd, neurodivergent, burned out, disabled,
  clinically unwell, high-potential, low-performing, or difficult.
- Whether a manager, team, organization, or adult is at fault.
- Whether a formal accommodation, health route, legal route, performance route,
  or disciplinary route is needed.
- Whether a role change must be permanent.

The output should be one or more carefully worded hypotheses, not a verdict.

## What Interpretation Can and Cannot Conclude

| Interpretation can say | Interpretation should not say |
|---|---|
| "This pattern suggests a possible autonomy fit gap." | "This person cannot handle structure." |
| "Learning may have flattened after fast mastery." | "This person is bored because they are superior." |
| "Quality concerns need a better route." | "The manager is anti-quality." |
| "Energy cost may be tied to meeting load and rescue work." | "This is burnout." |
| "A 30-day complexity lane is worth testing." | "The role must be redesigned around this person." |
| "The evidence is mixed; use a narrower experiment." | "The workbook proves the cause." |

Use "may indicate", "could be", "one hypothesis is", and "worth testing" when
the evidence is incomplete.

## Pattern-Based Interpretation Principles

1. Start with concrete work examples.
2. Look for repeated patterns across tasks, time, stakeholders, or role phases.
3. Separate what the adult experiences from what the role actually allows.
4. Treat mismatch as a person-role-context pattern, not as a character flaw.
5. Consider alternative explanations before choosing a hypothesis.
6. Prefer the smallest useful role-design test.
7. Protect privacy and disclosure boundaries.
8. Keep health, legal, occupational-health, disability, accommodation, and HR
   decision questions outside the workbook's conclusions.
9. Review after 30 days using observed evidence.

Avoid interpreting isolated comments as stable patterns. A single example can
start a conversation, but repeated examples are stronger.

## Distinguish Person Patterns From Context Patterns

Use this distinction to reduce blame and improve the experiment design.

| Question | More person-pattern evidence | More context-pattern evidence |
|---|---|---|
| Where does it happen? | Across many roles, teams, and tasks | Mostly in one role, manager relationship, team, or process |
| What changes the pattern? | Similar pattern despite different contexts | Pattern improves when role conditions change |
| What does the adult control? | Translation, timing, boundary-setting, request clarity | Decision rights, workload, meeting load, process rules, sparring access |
| What does the employer control? | Limited ability to change personal style | Role design, constraints, delegation, quality routes, focus time |
| What should be tested first? | A small adult-side communication or boundary change | A small role-context or management-process change |

Most situations contain both. Good interpretation asks:

> What can the adult reasonably adjust, and what can the role context reasonably
> adjust?

## Look for Misfit Without Blaming Either Side

Misfit is not the same as failure. A role can be legitimate and still not use a
person's capacity well. A person can have real strengths and still need better
translation, timing, or boundary habits.

Useful wording:

- "The current role design may not be converting this capacity into
  contribution."
- "The adult may need a clearer route to translate complex concerns."
- "The manager may need better decision-zone clarity to avoid unnecessary
  control."
- "The team may need a quality-dialogue format so critical thinking becomes
  usable earlier."

Avoid wording that implies:

- The adult is the problem.
- The manager is the problem.
- High complexity automatically deserves special treatment.
- The organization must redesign everything.
- The workbook has identified the true cause.

## Identify Contribution Blockers

Contribution blockers are conditions that prevent useful capacity from becoming
visible, trusted, translated, or sustainable.

| Blocker | What it can look like | First interpretation step |
|---|---|---|
| Low complexity | Routine execution, no problem-framing, no ambiguous work | Check whether a bounded complexity lane is possible |
| Low autonomy | Tight method control, unclear decision rights, repeated approval loops | Map decision zones and risk boundaries |
| Low learning | Fast mastery followed by repetition or volume | Identify a real-work learning sprint |
| Weak quality dialogue | Concerns framed as negativity or perfectionism | Separate issue, evidence, risk, trade-off, and decision |
| Weak sparring | No one challenges or develops the adult's thinking | Identify sparring route or peer review rhythm |
| Translation gap | Insight is too fast, broad, abstract, or poorly timed | Use one-page recommendation format |
| Energy overload | Meeting load, rescue work, switching, emotional load, no recovery | Protect focus time and define stop rules |
| Role ambiguity | Adult compensates for unclear ownership or constraints | Clarify outcomes, authority, and non-negotiables |
| Safety concern | Pressure, retaliation risk, adversarial dynamics | Pause ordinary use and involve support |

Do not jump from blocker to blame. The practical question is:

> Which blocker is small enough and important enough to test first?

## Identify Usable Role-Design Hypotheses

A good hypothesis is specific, work-based, and testable in 30 days.

| Weak interpretation | Better role-design hypothesis |
|---|---|
| "This person needs more challenge." | "If we add one bounded analysis lane to project X, contribution may become more visible without expanding workload." |
| "The manager micromanages." | "If outcomes, constraints, and decision zones are clarified, method freedom may increase where risk allows." |
| "The adult is too intense." | "If quality concerns use an issue-evidence-risk-trade-off format, intensity may become easier to use earlier." |
| "The role is boring." | "If a 30-day learning sprint is attached to a real stakeholder problem, engagement and useful output may improve." |
| "There is burnout risk." | "If focus time is protected and informal rescue work is paused, energy cost can be reviewed after 30 days." |

Each hypothesis should include:

- The fit dimension being tested.
- The work condition to change.
- The adult action.
- The manager or employer action.
- What will be paused, reduced, or protected.
- Evidence of improvement or harm.
- Stop conditions and review date.

## Handle Conflicting Signals

Conflicting signals are normal. They are a reason to narrow interpretation, not
to force certainty.

| Conflict | Possible meaning | Useful response |
|---|---|---|
| Adult sees low autonomy, manager sees high autonomy | Decision rights may be unclear or autonomy may exist only in low-impact areas | Map decision zones with examples |
| Adult reports underuse and overload | Capacity may be underused in the right work and overused in rescue or volume work | Separate contribution work from load sources |
| Manager sees quality concerns as resistance | Values or risk concerns may lack timing, evidence, or decision route | Use structured quality dialogue |
| Adult wants complexity, manager worries about focus | Complexity lane may need clear scope and workload protection | Define one bounded lane and pause something else |
| Work improved but energy worsened | Experiment may add hidden workload | Redesign or stop |

When signals conflict, ask:

> What small change would make the pattern more observable without creating
> unfair risk?

## Prevent Over-Interpretation

Over-interpretation happens when users turn reflection into proof.

Safeguards:

- Do not count signals as a score.
- Do not compare adults against each other.
- Do not infer diagnosis, IQ, motivation, health, or personality.
- Do not store private workbook content as performance evidence.
- Do not treat pathway hypotheses as fixed categories.
- Do not make permanent role decisions from one conversation.
- Do not ignore legitimate business constraints.
- Do not use the tool to avoid a necessary HR, health, legal, or
  occupational-health route.

Use this check before sharing an interpretation:

| Check | Yes/No |
|---|---:|
| Is the interpretation grounded in concrete work examples? |  |
| Does it include alternative explanations? |  |
| Is it framed as a hypothesis rather than a conclusion? |  |
| Is the adult's privacy protected? |  |
| Is it separate from performance, promotion, selection, or ranking? |  |
| Can it be tested with a bounded 30-day experiment? |  |

## Decide What Kind of Issue This May Be

The workbook is useful for role-design questions. It is not the right primary
tool for every situation.

| If the main pattern is... | It may be mostly... | First useful next step |
|---|---|---|
| Method control, unclear decision rights, fixed frames | Role design or management process | Map outcomes, constraints, and decision zones |
| Fast mastery followed by repetition | Learning and complexity fit | Create learning sprint or complexity lane |
| Repeated critical concerns with poor reception | Communication, quality route, or values conflict | Use quality-dialogue format and clarify escalation route |
| Constant rescue work and no recovery | Workload, boundaries, or role ambiguity | Pause or reduce load before adding an experiment |
| Sharp disagreement about facts or motives | Trust, facilitation, or relationship repair | Use neutral facilitator and observable evidence |
| Ethical, safety, fairness, or integrity issue | Values conflict or formal escalation issue | Clarify route; involve appropriate authority if needed |
| Symptoms, exhaustion, health, disability, or formal accommodation needs dominate | Broader health, occupational-health, or accommodation issue | Refer to appropriate professional route |
| Discipline, legal risk, grievance, or unsafe dynamics dominate | Formal HR/legal issue | Do not use ordinary workbook flow |

The workbook can support conversation around some of these issues, but it should
not replace the route that actually fits the situation.

## When to Slow Down, Involve Another Professional, or Avoid Use

Slow down when:

- Evidence is thin or mostly abstract.
- Adult and manager interpretations are far apart.
- The adult feels exposed or pressured.
- The manager wants a quick conclusion.
- The experiment would add workload without protection.
- The role constraints are unclear.

Involve a coach, HR partner, facilitator, occupational-health professional,
legal advisor, clinical professional, or accommodation specialist when the
actual issue requires that route.

Avoid ordinary use when:

- The situation is unsafe, adversarial, disciplinary, or legally sensitive.
- The employer wants scoring, ranking, selection, promotion, or performance
  evidence.
- The adult cannot freely choose what to disclose.
- Completed responses would be stored or reused without clear consent and
  purpose.
- A health, disability, accommodation, legal, or occupational-health decision is
  the real question.

## Interpretation Tables

Use the tables below as conversation aids. They are not scoring rules.

## Cognitive Complexity

| Possible signal | What it may indicate | Alternative explanations | Useful conversation question | Possible role-design implication |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| The adult quickly sees system patterns others miss | Complex thinking may be underused or poorly translated | Role may not need this level of analysis; timing may be poor | Where would this pattern recognition be useful if scoped well? | Add a bounded analysis, risk, or options lane |
| Routine tasks drain energy faster than complex tasks | Complexity fit may be low | Recovery, workload, or motivation may also be relevant | Which tasks are necessary routine, and which could be balanced with complex work? | Balance routine with one defined complex contribution |
| The adult challenges problem framing often | They may need earlier problem-framing access | They may lack context or underestimate constraints | Which decisions are still open for framing, and which are fixed? | Invite challenge earlier in selected forums |
| Complex ideas are ignored or seen as side comments | Contribution may be hidden or not translated | Ideas may be too broad, late, or not linked to decisions | What would make the idea usable for this decision? | Use one-page observation, implication, recommendation format |

## Learning Speed

| Possible signal | What it may indicate | Alternative explanations | Useful conversation question | Possible role-design implication |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| The adult masters new work quickly, then disengages | Learning fit may flatten fast | Workload, recognition, or unclear goals may also matter | What is the next real learning edge in this role? | Create a 30-day learning sprint tied to real work |
| More volume is added after fast mastery | Capacity may be converted into load rather than development | Volume may be temporarily necessary | What can be deepened rather than simply increased? | Replace some volume with deeper problem ownership |
| The adult seeks new domains or stakeholders | Growth may require broader exposure | The current role may have legitimate scope limits | Which exposure would help the role, not only the person? | Add bounded stakeholder, domain, or method exposure |
| Training does not solve the issue | Learning need may be work-embedded | Training may be mismatched or poorly timed | What does the work itself need to teach next? | Use project-based learning rather than generic course |

## Autonomy and Trust

| Possible signal | What it may indicate | Alternative explanations | Useful conversation question | Possible role-design implication |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| The adult reacts strongly to micromanagement | Method autonomy may be too narrow | Quality, risk, or trust history may require oversight | Which controls are risk-based and which are habit? | Clarify method freedom within risk boundaries |
| Decision rights are repeatedly disputed | Authority or alignment zones may be unclear | Communication or stakeholder politics may be driving confusion | Which decisions are mine, shared, or organizational? | Create a decision-zone map |
| Autonomy feels like abandonment | Support and alignment may be too low | Adult may expect more guidance than role provides | Where is independence useful, and where is alignment needed? | Add review rhythm without controlling method |
| Approval loops slow useful work | Process may be disproportionate to risk | Compliance, safety, or stakeholder needs may justify approvals | Which approvals protect real risk? | Remove or shorten low-risk approvals |

## Values Sensitivity

| Possible signal | What it may indicate | Alternative explanations | Useful conversation question | Possible role-design implication |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| The adult raises fairness, quality, ethics, or risk concerns repeatedly | Values and quality fit may be central | Concerns may be preference, perfectionism, or poor timing | What is the evidence, risk, trade-off, and decision needed? | Use a structured quality-dialogue route |
| The manager experiences concerns as negativity | Translation or timing may be weak | The work context may discourage dissent | How can concerns be raised early and usefully? | Define forum and format for critical concerns |
| "Good enough" feels unacceptable | Standards may need explicit trade-offs | The adult may not see business constraints | What makes this trade-off legitimate or risky? | Clarify good-enough criteria and escalation threshold |
| Values conflict creates exit talk | Role or organization fit may be strained | Temporary conflict, workload, or trust issues may amplify it | Which values issue is negotiable, and which is not? | Use facilitation or refer to appropriate route |

## Intensity

| Possible signal | What it may indicate | Alternative explanations | Useful conversation question | Possible role-design implication |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Work output is strong but energy cost is high | Contribution may rely on overdrive | Personal boundary habits or workload may also matter | What must be protected or reduced to make this sustainable? | Protect focus time and stop informal rescue work |
| Meetings and switching drain disproportionate energy | Focus conditions may be poor | Collaboration demands may be legitimate | Which meetings create value, and which create avoidable switching? | Run meeting/focus-time review |
| The adult becomes intense around risk or quality | Intensity may contain useful signal but need translation | Stress, timing, or trust issues may amplify it | What is the underlying work risk or decision? | Convert intensity into evidence and decision language |
| Recovery after intense periods is missing | Sustainability risk may be rising | Short-term peak may be expected and temporary | What recovery pattern is realistic after peak work? | Build recovery or workload reset into the experiment |

## Context Fit

| Possible signal | What it may indicate | Alternative explanations | Useful conversation question | Possible role-design implication |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| The same adult contributes well in one context and poorly in another | Context fit may be decisive | Task type, manager relationship, or team maturity may differ | What conditions are different in the better context? | Copy one enabling condition into the current role |
| Hierarchy creates repeated friction | Low explanation, low influence, or fixed authority may block contribution | Formal constraints may be legitimate | Which constraints are non-negotiable, and which can be discussed? | Separate necessary structure from avoidable process noise |
| Psychological safety is weak | Ordinary workbook use may be unsafe | There may be misunderstanding rather than safety risk | What would make discussion safe enough, if anything? | Use facilitation or pause |
| Role cannot absorb suggested contribution | Fit may not be redesignable enough | Timing or scope may be wrong | What role change is actually available? | Stop, narrow, or explore another role path |

## Sustainable Contribution

| Possible signal | What it may indicate | Alternative explanations | Useful conversation question | Possible role-design implication |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Contribution, energy, and collaboration all improve during an experiment | Role lever may be useful | Novelty effect or temporary attention may contribute | What evidence should we watch for another 30 days? | Continue or formalize the role lever carefully |
| Output improves but energy worsens | Experiment may add hidden workload | Short-term learning curve may be costly | What must be paused, reduced, or protected? | Redesign before continuing |
| Energy improves but contribution does not | Load reduction helped, but role fit may still be unclear | Evidence window may be too short | What contribution outcome needs clearer definition? | Refine experiment or add complexity/learning lever |
| No meaningful change after 30 days | Hypothesis may be weak or role may not be adjustable enough | Implementation may have been inconsistent | Did we actually run the experiment as agreed? | Adjust, stop, escalate, or choose another fit dimension |
