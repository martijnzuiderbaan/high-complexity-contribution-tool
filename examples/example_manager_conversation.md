# Example Manager Conversation

This is a fictional example for product demonstration only. It is not based on
a real person, employer, manager, client, or HR case.

The example shows how a manager conversation can stay practical,
manager-safe, and non-diagnostic while producing one 30-day role-design
experiment.

## 1. Fictional Setup

| Setup field | Example |
|---|---|
| Adult context | A senior product operations analyst has prepared selected workbook notes privately. |
| Manager context | The manager leads a delivery operations team and wants client responsiveness, predictable reporting, and fewer repeated escalations. |
| Shared goal | Test whether protected analysis time on one client stream improves contribution and reduces repeat rework without harming urgent response. |
| Conversation boundary | The meeting is not a diagnosis, performance review, promotion discussion, talent ranking, support-entitlement decision, or HR case review. |
| Documentation boundary | The manager documents only agreed role-design actions, review evidence, stop conditions, and the review date. Private workbook content is not stored. |

Opening frame:

> Let us use this conversation to inspect one work-context pattern and decide
> whether a small 30-day experiment is worth testing. We are not making a
> conclusion about you as a person or about employment decisions.

## 2. 30-Minute Conversation Version

Use this version only when preparation is already done and there is enough
trust for a concise conversation.

### Agenda

| Time | Focus | Output |
|---:|---|---|
| 0-5 min | Purpose, boundaries, and privacy | Shared frame |
| 5-10 min | Strongest useful contribution | Contribution statement |
| 10-15 min | Main fit gap and alternative explanations | Narrowed pattern |
| 15-25 min | Experiment design | 30-day agreement |
| 25-30 min | Review date, stop conditions, documentation | Follow-up plan |

### Example Dialogue Snippets

Manager:

> I understand you are not asking me to accept a label or make an HR decision.
> What is the work pattern you want us to look at?

Adult:

> The pattern is that I can often see recurring client issues across teams, but
> that analysis is squeezed between urgent escalations and meetings. I want to
> test whether a small protected analysis lane would make that contribution
> more useful.

Manager:

> What would stay unchanged during the experiment?

Adult:

> Client responsiveness stays important. I am proposing agreed escalation
> windows and clear interruption rules, not opting out of urgent work.

Manager:

> What would you adjust on your side?

Adult:

> I will use a one-page recommendation format so the analysis is easier to
> review: observation, implication, recommendation, risk of inaction, and
> decision requested.

Manager:

> What evidence should we review after 30 days?

Adult:

> Recommendation usefulness, repeated rework, meeting or switching load, any
> missed escalation risk, and whether hidden work appeared.

### Manager Questions

- Which concrete work example shows this contribution?
- Which part of the pattern is about role design, and which part is about
  timing or communication?
- What legitimate delivery constraint must remain protected?
- What is the smallest experiment we can test?
- What would count as harm or a reason to stop?

### Adult Responses

- Strong contribution: cross-team pattern analysis on recurring client issues.
- Main fit gap: no protected lane for analysis before decisions are fixed.
- Adult-side adjustment: one-page recommendation format and clearer decision
  request.
- Manager-side adjustment: protect two focus blocks and define interruption
  rules.
- Boundary: private workbook notes stay private.

### Notes

The 30-minute version should end with a draft experiment, not a complete
interpretation of the adult. If the conversation becomes rushed or sensitive,
stop at preparation and schedule a longer session with facilitation.

## 3. 60-Minute Conversation Version

Use this version when the manager and adult need more exploration or when a
coach, HR partner, or facilitator is present.

### Agenda

| Time | Focus | Output |
|---:|---|---|
| 0-5 min | Purpose, boundaries, privacy, and non-decision frame | Shared frame |
| 5-15 min | What is working | Strong contribution and enabling conditions |
| 15-25 min | Current work-context signals | Two or three concrete examples |
| 25-35 min | Alternative explanations and constraints | Narrowed interpretation |
| 35-48 min | Role-design exploration | Experiment elements |
| 48-55 min | Agreement points, stop conditions, documentation | Final plan |
| 55-60 min | Confirm owners and review date | Next actions |

### Deeper Prompts

- Where has this adult's contribution clearly helped the team or client?
- Which work conditions made that contribution possible?
- Which urgent work is legitimately fixed, and which interruption patterns may
  be avoidable?
- Where does the adult need earlier access to problem-framing?
- Which manager concern should stay visible during the experiment?
- What adult-side translation change would make the analysis easier to use?
- What would be too broad or too risky for the first 30 days?

### Role-Design Exploration

| Fit dimension | Discussion |
|---|---|
| Complexity | One client stream may provide enough ambiguity and systems thinking without expanding the whole role. |
| Autonomy | The adult can choose the analysis method within agreed output and timing boundaries. |
| Learning | The adult gains deeper exposure to product decision logic and client implementation patterns. |
| Values and quality | Quality concerns use a concise evidence and trade-off format. |
| Sparring and translation | The manager reviews one-page recommendations weekly and gives feedback on usefulness. |
| Energy and boundary | Routine escalation work is limited to agreed windows unless a defined urgent threshold is met. |

### Agreement Points

- The experiment tests a work-context hypothesis, not a personal conclusion.
- The adult will produce one-page recommendations for one client stream.
- The manager will protect two focus blocks per week where delivery risk
  allows.
- Escalation windows and interruption thresholds will be written down.
- The private workbook is not requested or stored.
- The review happens after 30 days and is about the experiment.

## 4. Manager Notes Template Filled In

Use this as an example of what can be documented. It is not a performance note.

| Field | Filled example |
|---|---|
| Strongest contribution observed | The adult identifies cross-team patterns behind repeated client issues and can translate them into practical recommendations. |
| Concrete example | Recent analysis of recurring handover issues helped the team separate reporting delay, ownership ambiguity, and client expectation gaps. |
| Possible blocked or underused capacity | Pattern analysis is often done late or after hours because urgent escalation and meetings dominate the schedule. |
| Current role constraints | Client response time, delivery commitments, and team coordination remain necessary. |
| Constraints that may be avoidable process noise | Unclear interruption rules and default meeting attendance may reduce focus time more than necessary. |
| Fit dimension that may matter most | Cognitive complexity combined with energy and boundary fit. |
| Possible adult-side adjustment | Use a one-page recommendation format and name the decision requested. |
| Possible manager-side adjustment | Protect two 90-minute focus blocks per week and clarify escalation windows. |
| Privacy or documentation boundary | Do not request or store private workbook content. Document only the experiment plan and review evidence. |
| Questions to ask, not conclusions to state | Did the protected analysis lane improve recommendation usefulness without harming client response or increasing hidden workload? |

### What the Manager Can Document

- The agreed role-design experiment.
- Adult and manager actions.
- Workload protection.
- Review evidence.
- Stop conditions.
- Review date.

### What Not To Document

- Private workbook responses.
- Diagnostic labels or test information.
- Health, burnout, disability, treatment, or occupational-health details.
- Full conflict narratives.
- Personal judgments about character, motivation, identity, or capability.
- Any performance, promotion, ranking, or support-entitlement conclusion.

### Action Commitments

| Owner | Commitment |
|---|---|
| Adult | Create one-page recommendations for the selected client stream before the weekly check-in. |
| Manager | Protect two focus blocks where possible and redirect non-urgent interruptions to agreed windows. |
| Both | Review workload, usefulness, and delivery risk weekly for four weeks. |

## 5. Joint Action Plan

| Element | Agreement |
|---|---|
| Role-design experiment | Protected client-pattern analysis lane for one client stream. |
| Owner | Adult owns the analysis output; manager owns scope, protection, and decision route. |
| Adult action | Use one-page recommendation format and flag the decision needed. |
| Manager action | Protect two 90-minute focus blocks per week and clarify what can interrupt them. |
| Boundaries | Private workbook stays private; no performance or promotion decision is attached; no extra analysis work without limiting routine escalation load. |
| Review date | 30 days after the experiment starts. |
| Success criteria | Clearer recommendations, reduced repeated rework, sustainable workload, no material client response harm, and improved decision clarity. |
| Evidence of no improvement or harm | Focus blocks repeatedly interrupted, urgent response worsens, hidden work increases, recommendations are not used, or the adult's energy cost rises. |
| Stop conditions | Delivery risk becomes too high, workload expands without protection, private disclosure pressure appears, or the conversation shifts into performance or HR decision framing. |

## 6. What Responsible Use Looks Like

| Good manager behavior | Risky manager behavior | Safer alternative |
|---|---|---|
| Asks for work examples and role conditions. | Asks whether the adult is officially gifted or has a diagnosis. | Ask which work pattern is relevant and what experiment could test it. |
| Documents the experiment and review date. | Stores private workbook responses in an HR file. | Document only agreed actions, evidence, and stop conditions. |
| Names legitimate constraints clearly. | Treats every constraint as fixed without explanation. | Separate non-negotiable constraints from process habits that may be adjustable. |
| Gives the adult method freedom inside agreed boundaries. | Controls the analysis method while still expecting ownership of the output. | Define outcome, constraints, and review rhythm, then leave method choices where risk allows. |
| Reviews whether the experiment worked. | Uses the review to rate the adult's performance. | Review contribution, clarity, energy, collaboration, and hidden workload. |
| Pauses when the conversation moves out of scope. | Continues into health, legal, disciplinary, or formal HR territory. | Use the appropriate support route for those issues. |

Responsible use keeps the conversation anchored in patterns, hypotheses, role
levers, and bounded experiments. It does not turn the tool into assessment,
diagnosis, performance management, or employer decision support.
