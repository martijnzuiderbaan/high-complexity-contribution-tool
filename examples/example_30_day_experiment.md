# Example 30-Day Experiment

This is a fictional example for product demonstration only. It is not based on
a real person, employer, manager, client, or HR case.

The experiment tests a work-context hypothesis. It does not test giftedness,
capacity, personality, performance, diagnosis, health, legal status,
occupational-health status, or suitability for promotion or support.

## 1. Experiment Purpose

The experiment tests whether a small role-design change helps complex
contribution become more visible, useful, and sustainable.

What the experiment tests:

- Whether one protected complexity lane improves recommendation usefulness.
- Whether clearer escalation windows reduce hidden rescue work.
- Whether a one-page recommendation format improves translation and manager
  decision clarity.
- Whether the change can run without harming client responsiveness.
- Whether workload and energy remain sustainable.

What the experiment does not test:

- Whether the adult is gifted, high-potential, underperforming, difficult, or
  deserving of special treatment.
- Whether the manager is right or wrong.
- Whether the adult should be promoted, rated differently, selected for
  another role, formally accommodated, or placed in a personnel process.
- Whether there is any clinical, legal, occupational-health, or disability
  issue.

Useful experiment statement:

> For 30 days, we will test whether two protected focus blocks per week for one
> client-pattern analysis lane improve recommendation quality and reduce repeat
> rework without increasing hidden workload or harming urgent client response.

## 2. Starting Hypothesis

| Hypothesis type | Starting hypothesis |
|---|---|
| Work-context hypothesis | The current role mixes urgent escalation and deeper improvement analysis in a way that hides higher-value pattern work and increases switching cost. |
| Contribution hypothesis | If analysis time is protected and connected to a clear decision route, the adult can produce more useful cross-team recommendations. |
| Risk hypothesis | If routine escalation work is not limited or shared, the experiment may add hidden workload and increase energy cost. |

Alternative explanations to keep visible:

- Delivery phase may genuinely require high responsiveness for the full month.
- The one-page format may not be enough if decision ownership remains unclear.
- The adult may need to narrow analysis further to make it usable.
- The manager may need stronger evidence before protecting this work longer
  term.

## 3. Experiment Design

| Element | Agreement |
|---|---|
| Change to role/design/communication/autonomy | Add one protected complexity lane for a selected client stream. The adult gets method freedom for analysis within agreed output, timing, and risk boundaries. |
| Duration | 30 days. |
| Adult action | Use two 90-minute focus blocks per week for analysis; produce one-page recommendations; flag the decision requested; report hidden workload early. |
| Manager action | Protect focus blocks where delivery risk allows; define interruption thresholds; give weekly feedback on recommendation usefulness; clarify decision route. |
| Communication format | One-page recommendation: observation, implication, recommendation, risk of inaction, decision requested. |
| Boundaries | Private workbook stays private; no diagnosis, rating, promotion, selection, or performance conclusion is attached. |
| Support needed | Weekly 15-minute check-in; manager feedback on usefulness; one agreed escalation window review. |
| What remains unchanged | Client response obligations, delivery commitments, team coordination, and legitimate urgent escalations remain active. |
| What is paused, reduced, or protected | Non-urgent escalation support is redirected to agreed windows; one recurring meeting may be skipped when it conflicts with a protected focus block; ad hoc analysis requests outside the selected client stream are deferred. |
| Evidence of improvement | Clearer recommendations, fewer repeated rework loops, better decision clarity, manageable workload, and no material client response harm. |
| Evidence of no improvement or harm | Focus blocks cannot be protected, recommendations are not used, urgent response worsens, hidden work increases, or energy cost rises. |
| Stop conditions | Delivery risk becomes material, workload expands without protection, private disclosure pressure appears, or the conversation shifts into performance or HR decision framing. |

## 4. Weekly Check-Ins

Each check-in should take about 15 minutes. The questions review the
experiment, not the adult's worth or capability.

### Week 1

Prompts:

- Did the focus blocks happen?
- Which interruptions occurred?
- Was the one-page format clear enough to use?
- Did any hidden workload appear?

Sample notes:

| Field | Sample note |
|---|---|
| What happened | One focus block happened fully; one was shortened by a client escalation. |
| Useful signal | The first one-page recommendation helped the manager see that two reported issues had the same ownership gap. |
| Risk signal | Interruption threshold was not specific enough. |
| Adjustment | Define "urgent enough to interrupt" before Week 2. |

### Week 2

Prompts:

- Did the revised interruption threshold help?
- Was routine escalation load contained?
- Did the analysis influence any decision?
- Was energy cost better, worse, or unchanged?

Sample notes:

| Field | Sample note |
|---|---|
| What happened | Both focus blocks happened. Two non-urgent interruptions moved to the agreed window. |
| Useful signal | Manager used one recommendation in a cross-team planning meeting. |
| Risk signal | The adult spent extra time polishing the recommendation. |
| Adjustment | Limit recommendation prep to 45 minutes after analysis and mark uncertainty instead of over-polishing. |

### Week 3

Prompts:

- Are recommendations becoming easier to use?
- Is the work still bounded to one client stream?
- Is client responsiveness intact?
- What should be stopped or reduced before Week 4?

Sample notes:

| Field | Sample note |
|---|---|
| What happened | Recommendation format became easier. Client response stayed within expected windows. |
| Useful signal | Team identified one recurring handover problem before it became another escalation. |
| Risk signal | Another team asked the adult to apply the same analysis to a second client stream. |
| Adjustment | Defer second client stream until the 30-day review. Do not expand mid-experiment. |

### Week 4

Prompts:

- Did the experiment run as agreed?
- What evidence is ready for the 30-day review?
- Did any stop condition occur?
- What should the adult and manager each prepare for the final review?

Sample notes:

| Field | Sample note |
|---|---|
| What happened | The experiment mostly ran as agreed. One focus block was lost to a genuine urgent issue. |
| Useful signal | Three recommendations were produced; two were used in manager decisions. |
| Risk signal | Meeting load remains high on two days per week. |
| Review preparation | Compare repeated rework in the selected client stream, manager usefulness rating, and adult workload notes. |

## 5. Review After 30 Days

| Review question | Example review note |
|---|---|
| What improved? | Recommendations were clearer and earlier. The manager could use two recommendations in planning conversations. One recurring handover issue was addressed before another escalation cycle. |
| What did not improve? | Meeting load stayed high on two days. The analysis lane was still vulnerable to genuine urgent client issues. |
| What was learned? | Protected focus blocks helped only when interruption thresholds were explicit. The one-page format reduced translation friction. Scope control was necessary because other teams quickly requested more analysis. |
| What to continue | Continue one protected analysis lane for the same client stream for another 30 days. Keep the one-page format. |
| What to stop | Stop accepting additional client streams inside the same focus blocks. Stop polishing recommendations beyond the agreed time box. |
| What to adjust | Move one recurring meeting away from the protected focus window and add a clearer weekly decision owner for recommendations. |

Review boundary:

> This review is about the experiment and role context. It is not a performance
> rating, promotion case, diagnosis, giftedness proof, or formal HR decision.

## 6. Decision Options

| Option | When to choose it | Example in this scenario |
|---|---|---|
| Continue | The experiment improved contribution and stayed safe enough, but more evidence is needed. | Continue the same client-pattern analysis lane for another 30 days. |
| Adjust | The hypothesis seems useful, but scope, workload, or communication needs refinement. | Move one recurring meeting and clarify weekly decision owner. |
| Try a different experiment | The first hypothesis did not produce enough useful change, but another role lever may fit better. | Test decision-zone clarity instead of protected analysis time. |
| Escalate to broader role conversation | The experiment shows a larger role-design issue that cannot be solved by a small change. | Discuss whether operations improvement work needs a formally defined percentage of the role. |
| Pause | The context is not safe enough, the experiment adds harm, or another support route is needed. | Pause if hidden workload grows or the conversation shifts into performance or HR decision framing. |

Decision chosen in this fictional example:

> Adjust and continue for another 30 days, with the same client stream, clearer
> meeting protection, and a weekly decision owner.

## 7. Example Final Summary

### Adult Perspective

> The protected analysis lane helped me turn recurring patterns into usable
> recommendations earlier. The one-page format made my thinking easier to use
> and reduced the need to explain everything verbally. The experiment also
> showed that scope control matters: if more client streams are added without
> reducing other work, the same pattern will become overload again.

### Manager Perspective

> The experiment gave me clearer recommendations and a better view of where
> recurring client issues came from. It worked best when interruption rules were
> explicit and when recommendations named the decision needed. I still need to
> protect delivery responsiveness and keep the analysis lane bounded.

### Joint Next Step

> Continue the experiment for another 30 days with one adjustment: protect the
> focus blocks more explicitly by moving one recurring meeting and naming a
> weekly decision owner. Do not expand to a second client stream until the next
> review.

What this summary does not say:

- It does not rate the adult's performance.
- It does not diagnose the adult.
- It does not prove giftedness or high potential.
- It does not make a promotion, hiring, support, legal, clinical,
  occupational-health, disability, or formal HR recommendation.
